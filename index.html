Chapter 1
Dropout
Plain English First
Imagine training a football squad of 100 players, but the same 11 always play. The starting 11 form rigid habits — Neuron A always covers for Neuron B. The other 89 never improve. Drop the ball: one player is injured on match day and the whole team collapses.

Dropout fixes this by randomly benching players before every practice. Today neurons 3, 7, 42 sit out. Tomorrow a different random set. Over thousands of sessions, every neuron learns to do its job alone. On match day (test time) you field the full squad — and get a superteam that was trained to be independently capable.
Why Dropout Is Crucial
Without dropout, deep networks with millions of parameters will memorise training data rather than learn the underlying function. They fit noise, achieve near-zero training loss, but fail completely on unseen examples. Dropout is the most cost-effective regulariser in deep learning — it adds zero parameters, costs nothing at inference, and implicitly trains an ensemble of 2ⁿ sub-networks simultaneously.
The problem: overfitting & co-adaptation
Problem 1 — Memorisation
An MLP with many parameters can perfectly fit training examples — including noise. It learns "by heart" instead of learning the pattern.

Train loss → 0, but test loss stays high.
L_train → 0 but L_test ↑
⇒ Generalisation gap
Problem 2 — Co-adaptation
Neurons learn to rely on each other in fixed ways. Neuron A always corrects for Neuron B's errors. They form a tight dependency. Remove one, and the whole prediction collapses.
How dropout works — every step
1
Compute activations normally
h = σ(Wx + b) — forward pass runs as usual. This gives the layer's output vector.
2
Sample a Bernoulli mask — one per neuron, independent
For each neuron i, flip a biased coin:
mᵢ ~ Bernoulli(1 − p)
mᵢ = 1 with prob (1−p) → keep
mᵢ = 0 with prob p → drop
The mask is re-sampled fresh on every single forward pass. No two training steps use the same sub-network.
3
Apply the mask — zero out dropped neurons
h̃ᵢ = mᵢ · hᵢ. A dropped neuron outputs 0 and vanishes entirely from the computation graph. Gradients do not flow through it.
h̃ᵢ = mᵢ · hᵢ
4
Inverted dropout — scale up surviving neurons
Only (1−p) fraction of neurons survive. Without correction, the expected signal magnitude shrinks by (1−p). We compensate by dividing by (1−p):
h̃ᵢ = (mᵢ / (1−p)) · hᵢ

E[h̃ᵢ] = (1−p)/(1−p) · hᵢ = hᵢ ✓
This scaling is done at train time so inference requires zero changes.
5
Test time: no dropout, full network
All neurons active, no mask sampled, no scaling needed. The full network approximates the average of all 2ⁿ sub-networks trained during the stochastic process. Output is deterministic.
h̃ᵢ = hᵢ (inference only)

Ensemble effect: With 6 neurons, there are 2⁶ = 64 possible sub-networks. Each forward pass trains one of them. At test time, the full network approximates all 64 predictions averaged together — a free ensemble.
Pros and cons
Advantages
  • Eliminates neuron co-adaptation
  • Zero extra parameters, zero inference cost
  • Implicit ensemble of 2ⁿ sub-networks
  • Works in MLPs and Transformers
  • One line in PyTorch: nn.Dropout(p)
Limitations
  • Slows convergence (noisy gradients)
  • Conflicts with BatchNorm — use one per layer
  • Less effective in CNNs (try SpatialDropout)
  • p needs tuning (typically 0.2–0.5)
  • Not helpful in very small networks
Chapter 2
Batch Normalisation
Plain English First
Picture a kitchen with 10 cooking stations in sequence. Station 1 preps ingredients and hands them to Station 2, which cooks and passes to Station 3. The problem: every time Station 1 tweaks her recipe, the temperature and seasoning of what she passes to Station 2 changes. Station 2 has to constantly re-adapt — and then Station 3 re-adapts to Station 2's changes, and so on. The entire kitchen is always re-adjusting upstream changes instead of doing its actual job.

BatchNorm installs a standardisation counter between each station. Before passing food forward, it rescales everything so it always arrives at roughly the same temperature and portion (mean ≈ 0, variance ≈ 1). Now every station can focus entirely on learning its own cooking job.
Why Batch Normalisation Is Crucial
As earlier layers update during training, the distribution of activations they produce keeps shifting — this is called Internal Covariate Shift. Later layers waste capacity constantly re-adapting to these upstream changes instead of learning the actual task. BatchNorm eliminates this waste, allowing much higher learning rates, making training dramatically faster, and acting as mild regularisation through the batch-level noise it introduces.
The problem: internal covariate shift
Internal Covariate Shift
As layer l updates its weights, the distribution of inputs to layer l+1 changes. Layer l+1 is always chasing a moving target — it spends capacity re-adapting rather than learning. This forces small learning rates and makes deep networks very hard to train.
Sensitivity to Initialisation
Without normalisation, early layers must be initialised with extreme care. Even slightly wrong starting values cascade into exploding or vanishing activations across many layers. This limits how deep networks can practically be.
How batch normalisation works — every step

Given a mini-batch B = {x₁, x₂, …, xₘ} of activations at a layer:

1
Compute batch mean
μ_B = (1/m) · Σᵢ xᵢ
Average activation across all m examples in the mini-batch.
2
Compute batch variance
σ²_B = (1/m) · Σᵢ (xᵢ − μ_B)²
3
Normalise — shift to zero mean, unit variance
x̂ᵢ = (xᵢ − μ_B) / √(σ²_B + ε)
ε ≈ 1e-8 prevents division by zero. After this step every activation is ~N(0,1).
4
Scale and shift — learnable γ and β
yᵢ = γ · x̂ᵢ + β
γ (scale) and β (shift) are learnable parameters. They let the network undo normalisation if a different distribution is optimal. Initially γ=1, β=0 (pure normalisation). During training they adapt to whatever distribution the next layer learns to prefer.
5
At inference: use running statistics
At test time there may be only 1 sample — you can't compute a batch mean. Instead BatchNorm uses exponential moving averages of μ and σ² collected during training. In PyTorch: model.eval() switches this on automatically.
Numerical example
Batch = [2, 4, 6, 8], γ=1, β=0
Batch x[2, 4, 6, 8]
μ_B(2+4+6+8)/4 = 5.0
σ²_B[(2−5)²+(4−5)²+(6−5)²+(8−5)²]/4 = 5.0
√(σ²_B + ε)√5 ≈ 2.236
x̂ᵢ (normalised)[−1.342, −0.447, +0.447, +1.342]
yᵢ = γx̂ᵢ + β[−1.342, −0.447, +0.447, +1.342] (γ=1, β=0)
After learning γ=2, β=3[+0.316, +2.106, +3.894, +5.684]
γ and β insight: With γ=1,β=0 the network is constrained to mean=0, var=1. With learnable γ=2,β=3 the network shifts and stretches the distribution to whatever it learned is best for the next layer.
Where to place BatchNorm in an MLP
Standard placement: Linear → BatchNorm → Activation
z = W·x + b
z̃ = BatchNorm(z) ← normalise before activation
h = ReLU(z̃)

Normalising before the activation ensures the activation function operates in its linear region, preventing saturation in sigmoid/tanh and keeping ReLU gradients healthy.

Pros and cons
Advantages
  • Dramatically faster convergence
  • Allows much higher learning rates
  • Reduces sensitivity to initialisation
  • Mild regularisation (batch noise)
  • Mitigates vanishing/exploding gradients
Limitations
  • Needs batch size ≥ 8 for stable statistics
  • Poor fit for RNNs (time-step dependency)
  • Train vs inference mismatch if eval() forgotten
  • Conflicts with Dropout in same layer
  • Slight memory and compute overhead
Chapter 3
Weight Initialisation
Plain English First
Before the concert starts, the sound engineer sets every fader on the mixing board to a starting position. If every fader is at maximum, the first note blows the speakers — exploding signal. If every fader is at zero, no sound comes out at all — vanishing signal. You need each fader at a sensible middle level so the first note is clear, audible, and in the right range.

Weight initialisation is setting those faders. In a 20-layer network, a signal passes through 20 multiplications. If weights average just 1.1× instead of 1×, after 20 layers you get 1.1²⁰ ≈ 6.7× amplification. At 0.9× you get 0.9²⁰ ≈ 0.12× — a vanished signal. Xavier and He initialisation find the exact safe starting range for each layer.
Why Weight Initialisation Is Crucial
The very first forward pass determines whether training can even start. Weights too large → activations explode → NaN → training crashes on step 1. Weights too small → activations vanish → gradients vanish → early layers never learn anything regardless of how many epochs you run. Good initialisation costs nothing (it's just a smarter random draw) and is the difference between training succeeding and failing before it begins.
Root cause: variance propagation through layers
Vanishing signal
Weights initialised too small. Each layer shrinks the signal. After k layers: h⁽ᵏ⁾ ≈ εᵏ → 0. Gradients vanish in backprop — early layers learn nothing.
Example: 0.1^20 = 10⁻²⁰ ≈ 0
Exploding signal
Weights initialised too large. Each layer amplifies the signal. After k layers: h⁽ᵏ⁾ ≈ Cᵏ → ∞. Gradients explode → NaN. Training crashes immediately.
Example: 2^20 = 1,048,576
The variance propagation equation
For z = Wx where W ∈ ℝⁿᵒᵘᵗ × ⁿⁱⁿ:

Var(zⱼ) = n_in · Var(W) · Var(x)

Goal: Var(z) ≈ Var(x) → need n_in · Var(W) = 1
→ Var(W) = 1 / n_in (forward constraint)
Xavier / Glorot initialisation — for tanh, sigmoid

Glorot & Bengio (2010) required variance to be preserved in both directions: forward pass (Var(W) = 1/n_in) AND backward pass (Var(W) = 1/n_out). Taking the harmonic mean gives:

Var(W) = 2 / (n_in + n_out)

Uniform: W ~ U(−√(6/(n_in+n_out)), +√(6/(n_in+n_out)))
Gaussian: W ~ N(0, √(2/(n_in+n_out)))

ReLU kills all negative activations — roughly half the neurons output zero every pass. This halves the effective variance. He et al. (2015) doubled the target variance to compensate:

ReLU kills ~50% of neurons per pass

Pros and cons
Advantages
  • Zero computational cost — just a smarter random draw
  • Prevents explode/vanish from step 1
  • Faster convergence from the very first update
  • Works synergistically with BatchNorm
  • Theoretically grounded derivations
Limitations
  • Derived assuming linear activations (approximate)
  • Less critical when BatchNorm is used
  • Mismatched init still hurts (Xavier + ReLU)
  • Doesn't fix architecture-level problems
Chapter 4
Regularisation
Plain English First
A student who only studied past exam papers can reproduce every answer perfectly — but the moment a question is phrased differently, they're lost. They memorised the questions, not the subject.

Regularisation is the teacher marking down for unnecessarily complicated answers. It adds a cost for complexity, forcing the model to find the simplest explanation that still fits all the evidence.

L2 (weight decay): soft ongoing tax — bigger weight, bigger cost. Weights shrink smoothly toward zero.
L1 (Lasso): flat entry fee — same charge regardless of weight size. Small weights get driven all the way to exactly zero, switching off irrelevant features entirely.
Why Regularisation Is Crucial
A neural network with more parameters than training examples can always achieve zero training loss — by memorising. Regularisation is the mechanism that prevents this. Without it, models trained beyond a certain point will learn the quirks of the training set rather than the general function, failing entirely on new data. L2 is the default in virtually all modern deep learning (via AdamW's weight_decay) precisely because it's always on, costs almost nothing, and consistently improves generalisation.
L2 regularisation (Ridge / weight decay)
L_total = L_data + λ · Σ Wᵢ²

Gradient: ∂L/∂W = ∂L_data/∂W + 2λW

Update: W ← W(1 − 2ηλ) − η · ∂L_data/∂W
Weight decay view: The factor (1 − 2ηλ) multiplies every weight at every step — slowly shrinking it toward zero unless the data gradient resists. Large weights are penalised more (gradient ∝ W), producing smooth, generalising solutions.

L1 regularisation (Lasso)
L_total = L_data + λ · Σ |Wᵢ|

Gradient (W ≠ 0): ∂|W|/∂W = sign(W) = ±1

Effect: constant force toward 0 → drives weights to exactly zero
Why sparsity? L2 gradient → 0 as W → 0 (weakens as weight shrinks). L1 gradient = constant ±λ regardless of weight size (never weakens). So L1 keeps pushing small weights past zero — switching them off entirely. Built-in feature selection.

Pros and cons
Advantages
  • L2: smooth, stable, theoretically clean
  • L1: automatic feature selection via sparsity
  • Both: directly reduce the generalisation gap
  • L2: trivial — just weight_decay in AdamW
  • Elastic Net: L1 + L2 combined
Limitations
  • λ must be tuned as a hyperparameter
  • L1: non-differentiable at 0 (subgradient)
  • L1: rarely effective in deep learning
  • L2 too large → underfitting
  • Not a substitute for more training data
Chapter 5
Optimisers
Plain English First
You are blindfolded on a hilly landscape. Your goal: reach the lowest valley. You can't see the whole terrain — only the slope under your feet right now. Your strategy for deciding which direction to step and how big each step should be is your optimiser.

GD: measure the entire map before one step — perfect direction, impossibly slow.
SGD: poke one random spot, step immediately — fast but wildly noisy.
Momentum: you're on a sledge — inertia smooths the zig-zag.
NAG: same sledge but you lean forward to look where you're heading before applying the brakes.
AdaGrad: shorter steps on well-worn paths, longer steps on rarely-visited cliffs — but never forgets, so eventually grinds to a halt.
RMSProp: AdaGrad with fading memory — only recent terrain counts.
Adam: sledge + adaptive footing + cold-start correction — the default.
AdamW: Adam with clean, decoupled weight decay — the modern standard.
Why Optimisers Are Crucial
The loss landscape of a deep network is a high-dimensional non-convex surface with saddle points, flat plateaus, narrow valleys, and many local minima. A naive gradient step stalls at saddles, zig-zags in valleys, and oscillates near minima. The choice of optimiser determines whether training converges at all, how fast it converges, and whether it finds solutions that generalise. Adam's combination of momentum and adaptive scaling is why modern networks with billions of parameters can be trained reliably at all.
Gradient Descent, SGD, Mini-batch SGD
GD Problem
Compute gradient over all N samples before each step. Accurate but impossibly slow for large datasets. One update per epoch.
SGD / Mini-batch Fix
Use 1 sample (SGD) or B samples (mini-batch) per step. Much faster. Noise in mini-batch gradient can actually help escape saddle points.
GD: g = (1/N)·Σ gᵢ, W ← W − η·g [1 update/epoch]
SGD: g = gᵢ, W ← W − η·gᵢ [N updates/epoch]
Mini-batch:g = (1/B)·Σⱼ gⱼ, W ← W − η·g [N/B updates/epoch]
Numerical example — N=4 samples, gradients [2, 4, 6, 8]
GD gradient(2+4+6+8)/4 = 5.0
GD update (η=0.1)W ← W − 0.1×5.0 = W − 0.5
SGD step 1 (g₁=2)W ← W − 0.1×2 = W − 0.2
SGD step 2 (g₂=4)W ← W − 0.1×4 = W − 0.4
Mini-batch B=2 step1g=(2+4)/2=3, W ← W − 0.3
Mini-batch B=2 step2g=(6+8)/2=7, W ← W − 0.7

Momentum
Problem it fixes
In narrow loss valleys, plain SGD oscillates side-to-side (zig-zag). It makes many costly sideways moves and very little net progress toward the minimum along the valley floor.
Solution
Accumulate a velocity vector that persists across steps. Consistent gradient directions build up speed. Opposing gradient directions cancel out. Zig-zag is damped, valley progress accelerates.
vₜ = β·vₜ₋₁ + (1−β)·∇L(Wₜ) [velocity — smoothed gradient]
Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − η·vₜ

β=0: no memory = plain SGD
β=0.9: strong memory, smooth path (typical)
3-step trace — W₀=0, η=0.1, β=0.9, grads=[2, 1.5, 1]
v₁0.9×0 + 0.1×2 = 0.2
W₁0 − 0.1×0.2 = −0.020
v₂0.9×0.2 + 0.1×1.5 = 0.33
W₂−0.020 − 0.1×0.33 = −0.053
v₃0.9×0.33 + 0.1×1 = 0.397
W₃−0.053 − 0.1×0.397 = −0.0927
Observationvelocity grows (0.2→0.33→0.397) — consistent direction builds speed
Advantages
  • Damps zig-zag oscillations
  • Faster convergence in valleys
  • Handles noisy gradients better
Limitations
  • Still evaluates gradient at current position
  • Can overshoot near sharp minima
  • Single global learning rate
Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG)
Problem it fixes
Classical momentum evaluates gradient at the current position — before the momentum step. If velocity is large, the method reacts late and overshoots turns or minima. It is reactive, not proactive.
Solution
Peek ahead first: project where momentum is about to take you, then evaluate the gradient there. This gives earlier warning that a minimum is approaching, so the method slows down before overshooting.
W̃ₜ = Wₜ − β·vₜ₋₁ [look-ahead position]
gₜ = ∇L(W̃ₜ) [gradient at look-ahead]
vₜ = β·vₜ₋₁ + η·gₜ
Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − vₜ

Momentum: evaluate gradient where you ARE
NAG: evaluate gradient where you're GOING
3-step trace — ∇L(W) = 2W+2, W₀=0, β=0.9, η=0.1
W̃₀, g₀0−0.9×0=0, g₀=2×0+2=2.000
v₁, W₁0.9×0+0.1×2=0.2, W₁=0−0.2=−0.200
W̃₁, g₁−0.2−0.9×0.2=−0.38, g₁=2×(−0.38)+2=1.240
v₂, W₂0.9×0.2+0.1×1.24=0.304, W₂=−0.200−0.304=−0.504
W̃₂, g₂−0.504−0.9×0.304=−0.778, g₂=2×(−0.778)+2=0.445
v₃, W₃0.9×0.304+0.1×0.445=0.318, W₃=−0.504−0.318=−0.822
ObservationLook-ahead gradients shrink (2→1.24→0.445) — NAG decelerates near minimum
Momentum says: move, then correct. NAG says: peek ahead, then move.
AdaGrad — per-parameter adaptive learning rates
Problem it fixes
All previous optimisers use one global η for every parameter. A parameter receiving large, frequent gradients (common word "the") overshoots. One receiving tiny, rare gradients ("tachycardia") barely moves. A single η can't be right for both.
Solution
Track the sum of squared gradients per parameter. Scale η inversely by √(accumulated sum). Frequently updated parameters automatically get smaller steps. Rare parameters keep a larger step.
Gₜ = Gₜ₋₁ + gₜ² [accumulated squared gradient]
Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − (η / √(Gₜ + ε)) · gₜ

Effective rate per param: ηᵢ = η / √(Gᵢₜ + ε)
Large grad history → small step (won't overshoot)
Small grad history → large step (won't stagnate)
Two parameters: W₁ gets large grads, W₂ gets tiny grads — η=0.1, ε=1e-8
g⁽¹⁾=[3, 0.01]G₁=[9, 0.0001]
Step W₁0.1/√9 × 3 = 0.1/3 × 3 = 0.100
Step W₂0.1/√0.0001 × 0.01 = 0.1/0.01 × 0.01 = 0.100
After 100 stepsG₁=900, G₂=0.01
ηeff for W₁0.1/√900 = 0.1/30 = 0.0033 ← tiny, slowing down
ηeff for W₂0.1/√0.01 = 0.1/0.1 = 1.0 ← still large
Fatal problemGₜ only grows → η → 0 → training freezes
Advantages
  • Per-parameter adaptive rate
  • Excellent for sparse features (NLP)
  • Eliminates per-layer η tuning
Fatal limitation
  • Gₜ only accumulates — never forgets
  • Learning rate → 0 → training freezes
  • Not suitable for deep networks
RMSProp — fixing AdaGrad's dying learning rate
Problem it fixes
AdaGrad's Gₜ accumulates all past squared gradients forever. After thousands of steps, Gₜ → ∞ and η/√Gₜ → 0. Every parameter's learning rate shrinks to zero. Training stalls.
Solution
Replace the growing sum with an exponential moving average (EMA) of squared gradients. Old gradients fade out with decay ρ. The denominator reflects only recent gradient magnitude — never explodes.
sₜ = ρ·sₜ₋₁ + (1−ρ)·gₜ² [EMA of squared grads, ρ≈0.9]
Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − (η / √(sₜ + ε)) · gₜ

AdaGrad: Gₜ = Gₜ₋₁ + gₜ² (always growing)
RMSProp: sₜ = ρ·sₜ₋₁ + (1−ρ)·gₜ² (bounded, fading memory)
3-step trace — W₀=1, η=0.1, ρ=0.9, constant g=2
s₁0.9×0 + 0.1×4 = 0.400
W₁1.0 − 0.1/√0.4 × 2 = 1.0 − 0.316 = 0.684
s₂0.9×0.4 + 0.1×4 = 0.760
W₂0.684 − 0.1/√0.76 × 2 = 0.684 − 0.229 = 0.455
s₃0.9×0.76 + 0.1×4 = 1.084
W₃0.455 − 0.1/√1.084 × 2 = 0.455 − 0.192 = 0.263
sₜ converges tog² = 4 (stable!) → ηeff = 0.1/2 = 0.05 stays constant
sₜ stabilises instead of growing forever. Once stable, the effective learning rate is constant. Unlike AdaGrad, training can continue indefinitely without the learning rate dying.
Advantages
  • Fixes AdaGrad's dying rate
  • Good for RNNs (Hinton's original use)
  • Works in non-stationary settings
Limitations
  • No momentum term
  • Biased EMA early in training (s₀=0)
  • No bias correction
Adam — momentum + adaptive scaling + bias correction
Problems it fixes
1. RMSProp has no momentum — it adapts step size but doesn't smooth direction.

2. Both mₜ and vₜ start at 0. Early estimates are severely biased toward zero — first steps are too small or erratic before the estimates warm up.
Solution
Combine: EMA of gradients (momentum, first moment mₜ) + EMA of squared gradients (RMSProp, second moment vₜ) + divide both by (1−βᵗ) to correct the zero-initialisation bias.
mₜ = β₁·mₜ₋₁ + (1−β₁)·gₜ [first moment — smoothed direction]
vₜ = β₂·vₜ₋₁ + (1−β₂)·gₜ² [second moment — recent magnitude]

m̂ₜ = mₜ/(1−β₁ᵗ) [bias-corrected direction]
v̂ₜ = vₜ/(1−β₂ᵗ) [bias-corrected magnitude]

Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − (η/√(v̂ₜ)+ε) · m̂ₜ

Defaults: β₁=0.9, β₂=0.999, ε=1e-8, η=0.001
Step 1 trace — W₀=0, defaults, g₁=0.5
m₁0.9×0 + 0.1×0.5 = 0.05
v₁0.999×0 + 0.001×0.25 = 0.00025
m̂₁ (bias-corrected)0.05 / (1−0.9) = 0.05/0.1 = 0.500 ← restored!
v̂₁ (bias-corrected)0.00025 / (1−0.999) = 0.25
Update0 − (0.001/√0.25)×0.5 = 0 − 0.001 = −0.001
Without bias corr.m̂₁ would be 0.05 instead of 0.5 → 10× too small
Why bias correction matters: At t=1, m₁ = 0.1×g₁ — just 10% of the gradient. Without correction the first step is 10× too small. The divisor (1−β₁ᵗ) = 0.1 at t=1 restores it to the full gradient. After ~50 steps, β₁⁵⁰ ≈ 0.005 so 1−β₁ᵗ ≈ 1 — correction has no effect.
Advantages
  • Momentum + adaptive scaling in one
  • Bias correction stabilises early steps
  • Robust defaults — rarely needs tuning
  • De facto standard in deep learning
Limitations
  • May find sharp minima → worse generalisation
  • L2 regularisation is distorted (use AdamW)
  • 2× memory: stores mₜ and vₜ per parameter
AdamW — decoupled weight decay
Problem it fixes
In Adam, adding L2 regularisation puts λW into the gradient. Adam then scales this by 1/√v̂ₜ — which varies per parameter and per step. The weight decay effect becomes inconsistent and unpredictable. It is not truly "weight decay" at all.
Solution
Apply weight decay directly to the weights after the gradient step — as a separate, constant multiplicative term. No scaling. Every parameter shrinks by exactly ηλ per step, regardless of gradient history.
Adam + L2: Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ − (η/√v̂ₜ+ε)·m̂ₜ − (ηλ/√v̂ₜ+ε)·Wₜ [distorted]

AdamW: Wₜ₊₁ = Wₜ(1−ηλ) − (η/√v̂ₜ+ε)·m̂ₜ [clean]

Decay term (1−ηλ) is constant per step — independent of v̂ₜ
Decay comparison — W=1, η=0.001, λ=0.01, v̂₁=0.25
Adam+L2 decayηλ/√v̂ = 0.001×0.01/√0.25 = 0.00002 (scaled by v̂)
AdamW decayηλ = 0.001×0.01 = 0.00001 (fixed, independent of v̂)
When v̂=0.001Adam+L2 decay = 0.001×0.01/0.032 = 0.000316 (10× larger!)
AdamW (same)Still 0.00001 ← constant, predictable
Why AdamW is now the default: Clean, uniform decay across all parameters. Better generalisation than Adam on virtually all benchmarks. Used in GPT, BERT, ViT, LLaMA. In PyTorch: torch.optim.AdamW(params, lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01).
Advantages
  • Correct, predictable weight decay
  • Better generalisation than Adam
  • State-of-the-art on most architectures
  • Drop-in replacement for Adam
Limitations
  • Inherits Adam's sharp-minima tendency
  • Same 2× memory overhead as Adam
  • Extra λ hyperparameter to tune

All key formulas — reference card
Dropout: h̃ᵢ = (mᵢ/(1−p))·hᵢ mᵢ~Bern(1−p) [train] | h̃ᵢ=hᵢ [test]
BatchNorm: x̂ᵢ=(xᵢ−μ_B)/√(σ²_B+ε), yᵢ=γx̂ᵢ+β
Xavier: Var(W)=2/(n_in+n_out) → W~U(±√(6/(n_in+n_out)))
He: Var(W)=2/n_in → W~N(0, √(2/n_in))
L2: L=L_data+λΣWᵢ², ∂L/∂W=∂L_data/∂W+2λW
L1: L=L_data+λΣ|Wᵢ|, ∂L/∂W=∂L_data/∂W+λ·sign(W)
Momentum: vₜ=β·vₜ₋₁+(1−β)·gₜ, Wₜ₊₁=Wₜ−η·vₜ
NAG: W̃=Wₜ−β·vₜ₋₁, gₜ=∇L(W̃), vₜ=β·vₜ₋₁+η·gₜ, Wₜ₊₁=Wₜ−vₜ
AdaGrad: Gₜ=Gₜ₋₁+gₜ², Wₜ₊₁=Wₜ−(η/√(Gₜ+ε))·gₜ
RMSProp: sₜ=ρ·sₜ₋₁+(1−ρ)·gₜ², Wₜ₊₁=Wₜ−(η/√(sₜ+ε))·gₜ
Adam: mₜ=β₁mₜ₋₁+(1−β₁)gₜ, vₜ=β₂vₜ₋₁+(1−β₂)gₜ²
m̂ₜ=mₜ/(1−β₁ᵗ), v̂ₜ=vₜ/(1−β₂ᵗ), Wₜ₊₁=Wₜ−(η/√(v̂ₜ)+ε)·m̂ₜ
AdamW: Wₜ₊₁=Wₜ(1−ηλ)−(η/√(v̂ₜ)+ε)·m̂ₜ
One-line intuitions
DropoutDon't let any neuron free-ride — make every one independently capable.
Batch NormKeep the signal at a healthy volume as it passes through every layer.
Weight InitStart every fader at a safe level so the first note is neither silent nor deafening.
L2 RegTax complexity — prefer smaller weights and smoother decision boundaries.
L1 RegFlat fee per feature — switch off everything that isn't genuinely useful.
AdamWMomentum + adaptive footing + cold-start fix + clean weight decay. Use this by default.
